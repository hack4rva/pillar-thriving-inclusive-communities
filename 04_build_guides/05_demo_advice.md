# Demo Advice - Thriving and Inclusive Communities

How to present your project compellingly and credibly in 3–5 minutes.

---

## The narrative structure that works

**Problem** (30 seconds): Show the specific friction. Use one of the user journeys from `03_artifacts/user_journeys.md`. Make the judge feel the pain.

**Insight** (30 seconds): The key thing you learned about why this is hard. For this pillar: "Trust is the barrier, not information. A service directory that looks like a government form will be ignored."

**Solution** (90 seconds): Show the tool working. Walk through a realistic scenario. Don't demo every feature — demo the single most compelling interaction.

**Impact** (30 seconds): Be specific and honest. "This tool could help the ~12,000 foreign-born Richmond residents who currently navigate these services without a safe, multilingual front door." Cite the census data if you used it.

**Continuation** (30 seconds): Name the champion. "We've talked to [Organization X] and they are interested in hosting this after the hackathon." If you haven't secured this yet, be honest: "Our next step is to connect with [Organization X]."

**Ask** (15 seconds): What do you need? "We need a community organization willing to maintain the data and an attorney to review the privacy statement."

---

## The trust framing

For this pillar, your competitive advantage in the demo is demonstrating that you understood the trust problem. Most tools for immigrant and refugee communities fail because they are technically functional but communally inaccessible.

Show that you thought about:
- Who is afraid to use this tool and why
- How the design addresses that fear
- What you explicitly did not build because it would create risk

Judges respond well to teams that show restraint and ethical judgment, not just technical capability.

---

## What to show in the live demo

For a **service finder** (Shapes A, B):
1. Open the landing page. Read the privacy statement aloud.
2. Select a category (housing).
3. Show 3–4 results with clear, plain-language descriptions.
4. Tap "call now" on one result.
5. Toggle to Spanish if you have it.

For a **messenger tool** (Shape C):
1. Show the admin interface where a service update is entered.
2. Show the WhatsApp message being sent (live or recorded).
3. Show how an ambassador would forward it.

For a **case manager tool** (Shape D):
1. Enter a search: "housing + Spanish speaker."
2. Show the filtered results with intake notes.
3. Show the referral note template copy function.

For a **pathway map** (Shape E):
1. Walk through the visual from left to right.
2. Highlight one friction point with specific data (e.g., "2-week wait for Social Services intake").
3. Explain what the map reveals that no one currently tracks.

---

## What to avoid in the demo

- Do not demo the admin or configuration side unless it is essential to the story
- Do not read the privacy statement in full — summarize it and say "the full text is available"
- Do not apologize for missing features — state what you built and frame scope decisions as intentional
- Do not claim the tool is ready for production or that it is authoritative
- Do not claim City endorsement unless you have it

---

## Likely judge questions and how to answer them

**"How do you keep the data current?"**
"Our current demo uses manually curated data. The maintenance model would be [X organization] updating a simple YAML/JSON file when information changes. We've designed for that. The next step is establishing that relationship."

**"How do you know residents will trust this?"**
"We designed the privacy statement and visual design specifically to address the trust barrier identified in the working session. We haven't done formal user testing with community members yet — that would be the first post-hackathon step, before going live."

**"Why not just improve Help1RVA directly?"**
"Help1RVA already exists and has the data. Our prototype demonstrates what a more accessible front end would look like. We think this is most valuable as a proposal to Help1RVA, not as a competing service."

**"What would it take to deploy this?"**
"Three things: a community organization willing to maintain the data, a legal review of the privacy statement, and multilingual content review by native speakers. We've designed the technical architecture to be low-maintenance."
