# C2 Services – Refugee Ecosystem Overview

*Prepared 2026-03-24* 

--- 

## Executive Summary

The refugee service ecosystem is a multi-layered network of inter-governmental, non-governmental, and private actors that deliver legal protection, health care, water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH), food security, education, information, and livelihood support. This document outlines the primary service providers, their core modalities, and their geographic reach as of early 2026. Key trends include the increasing reliance on Cash-Based Transfers (CBT) for food assistance, the digitization of information hubs to bridge service gaps, and the integration of mental health and psychosocial support into primary care frameworks.

## Introduction

## Legal & Protection Services

| Provider | Core Services | Geographic Reach | Key Indicator | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **HIAS** | Legal assistance (asylum applications, work authorizations, family reunification) | United States; programs globally | Provides free legal services to refugees and asylum seekers [1] | Data on exact numbers outside the U.S. varies by operational capacity. |
| **UNHCR – Operational Guidance (2022)** | Community-level health, protection, referral pathways | Global | Provides practical orientation for field staff [2] | Provides a template for national partners. |
| **EUAA – Country Guidance** | Legal analysis of asylum-related legislation, procedural safeguards | EU + EFTA states | Common analysis developed with senior-level policy officials [3] | Used by asylum officers and NGOs for case preparation. |

**Takeaway:** Legal aid remains concentrated in high-income host countries (U.S., EU). Low-resource settings rely heavily on UNHCR guidance; scaling national-level legal clinics is a strategic gap.

## Health & Psychosocial Support

* **World Health Organization (WHO) Refugee & Migrant Health Toolkit** – A consolidated repository of surveillance tools, training modules, and policy briefs designed to support efforts in strengthening refugee and migrant health care [4]. 

* **UNICEF – Child-Friendly Spaces (Makani centres)** – 139 centres operating in Jordan in host communities, refugee camps, and informal tented settlements delivering health, nutrition, and psychosocial activities [5]. 

* **CDC Domestic Guidance** – Outlines screening, vaccination, and mental-health protocols to promote and improve refugee health, prevent disease, and familiarize newly arrived refugees with the US healthcare system [6]. 

**Takeaway:** Integrated health packages that combine primary care, mental-health counseling, and vaccination are now the norm in large-scale operations, but many smaller camps still lack systematic mental-health services. 

--- 

## Water, Sanitation & Hygiene (WASH)

* **UNICEF WASH Programme** – Active in over 100 countries, providing access to clean water and reliable sanitation, and promoting basic hygiene practices that keep children safe [7]. 

* **Disability-Inclusive WASH in Jordan** – UNICEF and partners operate 139 child-friendly spaces (Makani centres) that incorporate accessible WASH services for refugees [5]. 

**Takeaway:** While coverage is high in urban refugee settings, remote camps often experience intermittent water supply; investing in inclusive and accessible infrastructure improves reliability for vulnerable populations.

## Food Assistance & Cash-Based Transfers

| Actor | Modality | Example Countries | Impact Metric |
|---|---|---|---|
| **World Food Programme (WFP)** | Cash-Based Transfers (CBT) | Somalia, Yemen, Afghanistan | Sends money to people where markets are functioning but people cannot afford to buy food [8]. |
| **WFP Supply Chain** | Emergency cash, retail engagement | Global | Supply chain assessments define the type of assistance and contract money transfer providers [9]. |
| **Local NGOs (e.g., Signpost)** | Digital information hubs | Global | Bridges the information gap for people in crisis, empowering communities [10]. |

**Takeaway:** CBT is increasingly preferred where markets function, as it reduces the cost of providing assistance and builds economic security [11] [12]. However, digital payment infrastructure remains a barrier in some rural host-community markets.

## Education & Child-Friendly Spaces

* **UNHCR-UNICEF Blueprint for Joint Action** – Over **two million** refugee children gained increased access to education, clean water, and child protection services since the initiative's launch [13]. 

* **USAHello – Education Guides** – Provides easy-to-understand translated information on life, education, and work in the USA for refugees and immigrants [14]. 

**Takeaway:** Multi-language digital guides and joint interagency blueprints dramatically increase school enrollment rates and child protection coverage.

## Information & Digital Help Hubs

| Platform | Description | Languages Supported | Reach / Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Signpost (IRC-led)** | Digital help centers offering location-specific service listings & translation | Multiple | Empowers people impacted by conflict, disasters, poverty, and violence [15]. |
| **Refugee.info (Signpost program)** | Country-specific asylum-procedure guidance, regularly updated | Multiple | Provided asylum seekers with accurate, translated information [16]. |
| **USAHello** | "One-stop" portal for life-in-USA resources (legal, health, education) | English, Dari, Pashto, etc. | Offers practical information and resources for newly arriving populations [14] [17]. |

**Takeaway:** Centralized digital hubs reduce information asymmetry; sustainability depends on continuous funding for translation and backend maintenance.

## Migrant Resource Centres (IOM)

* **IOM Migrant Resource Centres (MRCs)** – "One-stop shops" delivering safe and accurate information about migration procedures and services for migrants [18] [19]. 

* **Global footprint** – IOM supports the establishment and running of various MRCs globally, including notable hubs like the Migration Response Center in Poi Pet, Cambodia [19] [20]. 

**Takeaway:** MRCs are effective at early-stage information dissemination but require strong linkages to long-term protection services to ensure continuity of care. 

--- 

## EUAA Country Guidance

* The **European Union Agency for Asylum (EUAA)** has a mandate to produce Country of Origin Information (COI) reports relevant to the EU+ asylum caseload [21]. 

* Practical tools and guides help increase the knowledge and technical skills of asylum practitioners across the EU, aiding in the interpretation of legal standards [22] [23]. 

**Takeaway:** EUAA guidance is a critical, evidence-based resource for harmonizing asylum adjudication across member states. 

--- 

## HIAS Legal Services (U.S.)

* **Legal Services** – HIAS provides free legal services to help refugees and asylum seekers obtain work authorizations, asylum grants, green cards, and family reunification [1]. 

* **Resettlement Partners** – A network of local Jewish and secular service agencies working on the ground in communities across the country to help refugees resettle [24]. 

**Takeaway:** HIAS demonstrates a scalable partnership model; replicating the "partner-network" approach expands legal-service capacity efficiently. 

--- 

## USAHello Integrated Resources

* **Public-Benefits Guide** – Outlines eligibility for public benefits and additional services offered by the Office of Refugee Resettlement (ORR) [25]. 

* **Mental-Health Services Directory** – Provides a helpful list of mental health services for immigrants and refugees, including types of services and phone numbers to call [26]. 

**Takeaway:** Consolidating benefit-eligibility information with mental-health referrals improves holistic integration for newly arrived populations.
