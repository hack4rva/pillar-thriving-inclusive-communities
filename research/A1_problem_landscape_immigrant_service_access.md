# Richmond Immigrant Access: Trust Gaps, Not Service Gaps

## Executive summary — Trust and enforcement fears—not information—block access in Richmond

For immigrant and refugee residents in Richmond, Virginia, the primary barrier to accessing services is not a lack of information, but a profound deficit of trust and safety. A visible enforcement climate and lingering public-charge fears make privacy and eligibility the real chokepoints. In 2022, 17% of adults in immigrant families with children and 25% in mixed-status families avoided noncash government benefits due to green card concerns [1] [2]. By 2023, 8% of immigrant adults and 27% of those who are likely undocumented avoided applying for food, housing, or health care assistance because they did not want to draw attention to their immigration status [3]. 

These chilling effects mean that any software tool designed to bridge the access gap must prioritize safe, anonymous navigation and community-based organization (CBO) mediated handoffs. Treating privacy and perceived immigration risk as the primary access barriers is essential; tools must feature explicit data protection messaging and co-branding with trusted local entities rather than government agencies.

## Richmond service landscape: who serves whom, and on what terms

Major providers in the Richmond area serve specific slices of the immigrant population, often leaving many outside of eligibility based on their specific legal status or financial situation.

### CCC refugee resettlement: status-limited services and referral-centric intake

Commonwealth Catholic Charities (CCC) provides robust resettlement services, including housing, medical liaison, and case management [4]. However, eligibility is strictly limited to specific statuses: Refugees, Asylees, Cuban/Haitian entrants, Amerasians, Special Immigrant Visa holders, and Victims of trafficking [5]. Furthermore, their intake process relies heavily on a structured referral form that asks for detailed client information, assuming a referrer is mediating the process [5]. 

### Health Brigade: inclusive mission with concrete eligibility gates

Health Brigade offers primary care to low-income and uninsured residents in the Greater Richmond area [6]. While they do not discriminate based on citizenship or national origin [7], their eligibility gates are concrete: patients must be uninsured, have lived in their Virginia service area for at least 6 months, and have an income between 139-200% of the Federal Poverty Line [7]. Intake requires a registration appointment, a photo ID (which can be expired or foreign), and two months of income documentation, with an annual renewal requirement [7]. Notably, they do offer free HIV, Hep C, and STI testing to anyone, regardless of these categories [7].

### Curation layer (ReEstablish Richmond; VirginiaNavigator)

Information about these services exists but is often scattered. Organizations like ReEstablish Richmond curate health resources and direct patients to clinics like Health Brigade [8]. Platforms like VirginiaNavigator list programs such as CCC's refugee resettlement, noting that while refugee services require specific immigration statuses, interpreter services are "unrestricted" [9].

## Trust and fear as primary access barriers, not knowledge gaps

Chilling effects from public-charge anxieties and courthouse enforcement depress help-seeking across systems in Richmond. The fear of data exposure is a functional barrier that prevents residents from initiating contact.

### Public-charge chilling effects persist post-rule change

Despite the reversal of the Trump-era public charge regulation in 2021, immigrant families continue to express reluctance to participate in public programs [1] [2]. The Urban Institute found that one in six adults in immigrant families with children avoided noncash government benefits in 2022 because of green card concerns [1]. This avoidance implies that between three and four million children were in families avoiding benefits [1]. Expect voluntary under-disclosure and avoidance of official channels to remain high.

### Virginia courthouse incidents intensify local distrust

Local enforcement incidents amplify system-wide avoidance. The ACLU of Virginia reported ICE detentions at the Chesterfield County Courthouse in June 2025 [10] [11] and a similar incident at a Charlottesville courthouse in April 2025 [12]. These actions create a chilling effect, especially for victims of crime and witnesses [12]. National data has shown that such enforcement can lead to a 1,200% increase in courthouse arrests (as seen in New York in 2017), causing immigrants to avoid the justice system entirely [13]. This trust deficit spills over into health, legal, and social services.

## Friction mapping across the access funnel

The steepest drop-offs happen at eligibility verification and safe contact initiation, not at basic discovery.

| Stage | Evidence | Severity (H/M/L) | Why it matters | Suggested lever |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Discover services | CBO directories exist (ReEstablish; Navigator) [8] [9] | M | Info is scattered; LEP navigation challenges | Build multilingual, status-aware directory |
| Understand eligibility | CCC status limits [5]; HB FPL/residency/ID docs [7] | H | Many residents disqualified or uncertain | Eligibility triage + clear alternatives |
| Intake requirements | HB registration appt, ID+income, annual renewal [7] | H | Docs and phone workflows deter/delay | Prep checklists, proxy upload, reminders |
| Safe contact | Phone-first; referrer-centric forms [5] [7] | H | Fear of voice calls/traceability | Asynchronous encrypted messaging/SMS |
| Ongoing trust | ICE courthouse incidents [10]; public-charge fear [1] | H | Avoidance across systems | Privacy-first design; CBO co-branding |

*Key Takeaway:* While discovery tools are helpful, interventions must focus heavily on demystifying eligibility and providing safe, untraceable methods for initial contact.

## Services exist vs accessible: eligibility, documentation, and contact channels

Status and insurance screens—and how contact is made—determine real access. "Services exist" does not mean they are accessible to the average undocumented or mixed-status family.

| Provider/Program | Who is eligible | Key intake requirements | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Commonwealth Catholic Charities (Refugees) | Refugee, asylee, SIV, trafficking survivor, etc. [5] | Referral form; office selection [5] | Status-bound; robust services [4] |
| Health Brigade (Primary care) | Uninsured; 139-200% FPL; 6+ months area resident [7] | Photo ID; 2 months income docs; phone reg; annual renewal [7] | Free STI/HIV/HepC testing for all [7] |

*Key Takeaway:* A resident may find a clinic, but if they lack two months of income documentation or fear submitting a photo ID, the service remains inaccessible.

## What happens when residents give up: downstream consequences

Non-engagement increases violence risk, worsens health, and deepens child hardship. When immigrants fear interacting with public systems, the downstream consequences are severe. Following visible ICE presence in Denver courthouses, 13 women decided not to pursue domestic violence cases against their abusers for fear of deportation [13]. Nationally, advocates reported a 40% decrease in cases filed for immigrant crime survivors in 2017, with many clients staying with or returning to abusers [13]. Furthermore, the avoidance of safety net programs affected an estimated three to four million children in 2022, exposing them to greater risks of material hardship [1]. 

## Language access: policy backbone vs frontline variability

Virginia Medicaid mandates interpreters, but execution is uneven at public touchpoints. The Virginia Department of Medical Assistance Services (DMAS) 2025 Language and Disability Access Plan requires that limited English proficiency (LEP) individuals be made aware of free language assistance services and that these services be provided in a timely manner [14]. However, the plan explicitly notes a lack of data regarding phone contacts made to the Department of Social Services (DSS) call centers and local offices [14]. This indicates that while the policy backbone exists, frontline consistency cannot be assumed.

### Inferences (clearly labeled)
* *Inference:* Phone-first and referrer-centric workflows deter LEP and undocumented residents. (Based on Health Brigade's phone scheduling requirement and CCC's organizational referral design).
* *Inference:* Execution of language access varies significantly across local offices. (Based on DMAS-acknowledged data gaps regarding DSS interactions).

### Unknowns (publicly unverifiable now)

* The extent to which secure messaging channels (like WhatsApp) are currently utilized by local providers.

## Assessment: primary friction points in service discovery

Discovery is less of a hard barrier than trust and eligibility, but LEP and scattered information still cause misses. The primary friction points in Richmond rank as follows:
1. **Trust/safety (Highest):** Shaped by local ICE courthouse incidents and national public-charge chilling data.
2. **Intake/eligibility documentation:** Gathering acceptable IDs and income proofs.
3. **Safe contact initiation:** The reliance on phone-first workflows.
4. **Understanding eligibility rules:** Navigating complex status and income requirements.
5. **Discovery:** Finding the services in the first place (still meaningful for LEP users).

## Intervention mapping: what software can solve vs human/policy levers

Software can de-risk discovery, comprehension, and first contact; human navigators and policy change are needed for eligibility and trust repair.

| Friction point | Software tool can… | Human navigator needed to… | Policy/advocacy needed to… |
|---|---|---|---|
| Trust/safety fears | Provide anonymous mode; clear data minimization; panic/safe-exit; co-brand with CBOs | Vouch for tool; build relationships; safety-plan | Limit courthouse enforcement; sustain federal guidance on public charge |
| Eligibility confusion | Status-aware screening; show alternatives; doc checklists | Tailor complex cases; coach on exceptions | Simplify eligibility; harmonize documentation standards |
| Documentation burden | Secure, local-only doc prep; examples of acceptable IDs; reminders | Help gather proofs; serve as proxy | Broaden accepted documents; reduce re-reg frequency |
| Phone-first contact | Async messaging/SMS with translations; schedule windows | Do warm handoffs; follow-ups in user’s language | Fund non-phone channels; require accessible contact options |
| Language access gaps | Multilingual content; interpreter request scripts | Advocate when access fails | Enforce Title VI/Section 1557 compliance; resource interpreters |

*Key Takeaway:* A software solution must be paired with human navigation to be truly effective, acting as a secure bridge rather than a standalone fix.

## Product design principles for a safe, trusted Richmond tool

Privacy-by-default, low-barrier entry, and CBO partnership are non-negotiable. 
* **No-login browsing:** Users must be able to explore services without creating an account.
* **Explicit privacy "nutrition label":** Clearly state that no data is stored or shared with government entities.
* **Status-aware routing:** Filter out dead ends by asking optional, anonymous questions about eligibility criteria.
* **Proxy workflows:** Allow trusted community members or faith leaders to initiate contact on behalf of a resident.
* **Crisis links:** Embed immediate, safe exit pathways for domestic violence or trafficking situations.

## Stakeholders and partnership levers

Partner with CCC, Health Brigade, ReEstablish Richmond, ACLU-VA, and DMAS Civil Rights to align trust, content accuracy, and escalation. Concrete asks should include co-branding the tool to leverage existing community trust, forming a navigator beta cohort to test workflows, and establishing an escalation Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) with the DMAS Civil Rights Coordinator for when language access fails [14].

## Measurement plan: proving access, not clicks

Track movement through the funnel safely and ethically. Metrics should focus on anonymous discovery to eligibility clarity rates, the number of initiated contacts via safe channels, and successful document preparation. Privacy is paramount: all metrics must be measured in aggregate with absolutely no Personally Identifiable Information (PII) stored.

## Pilot plan and risk management

Start with low-barrier health/testing and DV/legal aid pathways to mitigate the "tool as surveillance" risk. An 8-week pilot focusing on Health Brigade's free testing services [7] paired with legal partners can establish initial trust. Risks include community mistrust and data exposure concerns. Mitigations must include a third-party privacy audit, the availability of offline/printable versions of checklists, and a community oversight board to guide deployment.
