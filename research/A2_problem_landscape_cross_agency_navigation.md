# One Front Door, Many Doors: Fixing Cross-Agency Navigation for Immigrant and Refugee Richmonders

## Executive Summary

Richmond can significantly reduce cross-agency friction for immigrant and refugee residents by enforcing existing language access policies, front-loading "no-doc" services, and standardizing closed-loop referrals. The current ecosystem forces vulnerable residents to repeatedly prove their eligibility and retell their trauma across multiple disconnected agencies. 

However, the policy infrastructure to solve this already exists. The City's Language Access Policy (AR 5.24) explicitly guarantees free interpretation and translation services [1] [2]. Virginia's Workforce Innovation and Opportunity Act (WIOA) guidance permits many career and support services to begin before work authorization is verified [3]. Furthermore, a statewide closed-loop referral network, Unite Virginia (powered by Unite Us), is already active in Richmond and has connected tens of thousands of Virginians to care [4] [5]. 

The strategic imperative is not to build new systems, but to operationalize and connect the existing ones. By deploying a standardized "Core Eligibility Pack," mandating Unite Us for cross-agency hand-offs, and training frontline staff on trauma-informed data minimization, Richmond can transform a fragmented bureaucracy into a cohesive support ecosystem.

## Problem Landscape for Immigrant and Refugee Residents

Multiple agencies, duplicative intakes, and documentation traps drive significant drop-off for immigrant and refugee residents seeking stability. The first two to three hand-offs are where most attrition occurs, with language barriers and documentation requirements acting as the primary accelerants.

### Where Residents Get Lost: First-Mile Intake and Referral Gaps

When a recently arrived family seeks assistance, they must navigate a maze of distinct entities: the Department of Social Services (DSS) for benefits, the Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority (RRHA) or Continuum of Care (CoC) for housing, Virginia Career Works for employment, and various nonprofits for reentry or legal aid. Each node requires its own intake process. Residents frequently get stuck at the "first mile" because they lack immediate proof of work authorization, a Social Security Number (SSN), or translated documents. Without a unified referral system, residents are handed paper lists of resources and forced to start from scratch at the next agency, repeating their stories and risking retraumatization.

### Evidence of Risk: HUD Litigation and FOIA-Sensitive Case Notes

The cost of weak language access is high. Richmond's current Language Access Plan (LAP) was developed partly in response to federal litigation and U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) complaints regarding code enforcement at mobile home parks, which disproportionately affected Spanish and Mixteco speakers [2]. Furthermore, current WIOA guidelines mandate that case notes become part of a participant's permanent record and are subject to Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) requests [3]. If agencies over-document trauma narratives during intake, they expose vulnerable residents to severe privacy risks.

## Richmond's Language Access Infrastructure

Free interpreters, iSpeak tools, and mandated department liaisons exist; compliance is the unlock. Administrative Regulation (AR) 5.24 gives the Office of Immigrant and Refugee Engagement (OIRE) the authority to implement a citywide Language Access Plan [1]. Success now depends on consistent frontline execution.

### AR 5.24 and OIRE Tools: What is Guaranteed Today

Effective July 1, 2023, AR 5.24 mandates that all City of Richmond departments provide bilingual or multilingual services to ensure meaningful access for individuals with Limited English Proficiency (LEP) [1]. The city's official policy states: "All City of Richmond residents will be provided an interpreter at no charge. The City does not require, request, or prefer that residents bring their own interpreter" [2]. To support this, OIRE provides the "iSpeak Richmond" portal, which includes language identification guides and over-the-phone interpretation cards for city employees [6]. 

### Training and Enforcement: Building Cultural Competency

OIRE offers critical training courses for city employees, including "How to Work with Interpreters," "Cultural Awareness," and "iSpeak Richmond Orientation" [7]. These courses are designed to instruct staff on protocols, ethical issues, and compliance with the HUD-COR Settlement Agreement [7]. 

## Workforce and Benefits Intake Realities

Agencies can use WIOA flexibilities to deliver services before full documentation is secured. Virginia's 2025 WIOA guidance explicitly aims to reduce administrative barriers, allowing services to begin while work authorization and SSNs are pending [3].

### Services That Do Not Require Work Authorization

Under WIOA guidelines, staff can provide several services without verifying work authorization to ensure efficient delivery to workers in need [3]. 

| Service Category | Examples of Permitted Services Without Work Authorization |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Labor Exchange** | Career exploration, resume assistance, job search assistance [3]. |
| **Information & Referrals** | Worker rights info, referrals to food, housing, and medical assistance [3]. |
| **Individualized Services** | Career assessments, group counseling, one-on-one case management [3]. |
| **Education** | Basic skills education, English language instruction, high school equivalency [3]. |

*Key Takeaway*: Agencies can immediately engage immigrants in ESL and career planning while waiting for Employment Authorization Documents (EADs). Work authorization is only strictly required for job placement, occupational training, and direct financial supportive services [3].

### Self-Attestation, Pseudo-SSNs, and Priority of Service

Documentation barriers can be mitigated using existing policy flexibilities. While staff must request an SSN for reporting, they cannot deny services if an individual declines to provide one; instead, they can use an alternate unique identifier (pseudo-SSN) [3]. Furthermore, self-attestation is an acceptable source of documentation for almost all program elements related to WIOA eligibility (excluding work authorization, selective service, and basic skills deficiency) [3]. 

### Selective Service: Avoiding Denials for Men 26+

A hidden tripwire exists for male immigrants. All males residing in the U.S. (including undocumented immigrants, refugees, and asylees) between 18 and 25 must register for Selective Service [3]. For men 26 and older who failed to register, agencies must determine if the failure was "knowing and willful" [3]. If deemed knowing and willful, WIOA services must be denied [3]. Staff can help clients request a Status Information Letter (SIL) and document evidence to prove the failure was not intentional [3].

### Cross-Program Documentation Overlaps

| Eligibility Element | WIOA Requirement | DSS/Housing Requirement | Alignment Opportunity |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Age/Identity** | Driver's license, passport, school ID, self-attestation [3] | *To be validated* | Standardize on self-attestation where permitted. |
| **Income** | Pay stubs, bank statements, SNAP/TANF receipt, self-attestation [3] | *To be validated* | Use SNAP/TANF receipt as automatic WIOA low-income qualifier [3]. |
| **Work Authorization** | I-9 List A, or List B + C; SAVE system [3] | *To be validated* | Create a shared "Core Eligibility Pack" checklist. |

*Key Takeaway*: Because WIOA accepts SNAP or TANF receipt as automatic proof of low-income status [3], integrating DSS data with workforce intake can eliminate redundant income verification.

## Referral and Data-Sharing Infrastructure

Unite Virginia is the ready-made, closed-loop backbone for Richmond. A single consented referral system already operates across health and community partners and can standardize hand-offs.

### What Exists: Richmond City Navigator and Statewide Usage

Unite Us provides the enterprise technology for Unite Virginia, a coordinated care network linking healthcare, government, and community partners [4] [8]. In Richmond, residents can use a specific Food & Social Care Resource Navigation form, which promises contact from a staff member within two business days [9]. The Virginia Hospital & Healthcare Association (VHHA) has partnered with Unite Us since 2019, connecting 68,353 individuals to social services across the Commonwealth [5]. Local health departments, such as the Roanoke Health District, already use Unite Us for Community Health Worker referrals [10].

### Privacy-by-Design and Network Onboarding

Unite Us operates on a consent-based model, ensuring information is completely confidential and only shared to connect individuals to services [9]. This aligns with WIOA mandates to safeguard protected information and keep medical/disability data out of general case notes [3].

| Agency / Partner | Referral Use-Case in Unite Us |
| :--- | :--- |
| **OIRE / iSpeak** | Initial triage, language access dispatch, cultural support. |
| **City DSS** | Benefits enrollment (SNAP, TANF, Medicaid, RCA). |
| **Virginia Career Works** | WIOA enrollment, ESL classes, job placement. |
| **Housing / CoC** | RRHA waitlist navigation, emergency shelter. |

*Key Takeaway*: Mandating Unite Us across these core agencies will replace paper referrals with trackable, closed-loop connections.

## Typical Service Pathway Map

A standardized route prevents looping and ensures residents move logically from triage to stability.

**Ideal State Pathway:**
1. **OIRE / iSpeak Intake**: Initial contact, language identification, and interpreter dispatch.
2. **DSS / CommonHelp**: Benefits screening (SNAP/TANF/Medicaid/RCA).
3. **Virginia Career Works**: "No-docs" career services, ESL assessment, and SSN/Selective Service triage.
4. **Housing Navigation**: RRHA waitlist entry or CoC homelessness response.
5. **Legal / Community Support**: Immigration paperwork and reentry services.
6. **Follow-up**: All hand-offs tracked and closed via the Unite Us platform.

## Repeating-Your-Story: Root Causes and Fixes

Language, consent, and documentation design determine whether stories travel or are re-told. 

### Causes of Friction
The primary causes of the "repeating your story" problem are the lack of closed-loop referrals, inconsistent use of professional interpreters (relying instead on family members), and overlong, unstructured case notes that fail to highlight core eligibility facts [3] [2].

### Solutions
Agencies must replace narrative re-collection with a reusable "Core Intake Snapshot." By logging interpreter offers systematically and requiring Unite Us for all cross-agency referrals, providers can share the minimum necessary data to close a referral without forcing the client to recount their trauma.

## Metrics and Governance

To know if friction is falling, Richmond must track specific operational KPIs:
* **Time-to-First-Contact**: Tracked via Unite Us (target: < 2 business days) [9].
* **Closed-Loop Referral Rates**: Percentage of referrals that result in delivered services [8].
* **Interpreter Utilization**: Tracked by department to ensure compliance with AR 5.24 [1].
* **Denial Reductions**: Tracking the drop in WIOA denials related to Selective Service or SSN documentation [3].

## Risks, Constraints, and Legal Guardrails

Title VI compliance, FOIA exposure, and eligibility safeguards require trauma-informed, minimum-necessary design. The City's LAP is a legal necessity tied to Title VI of the Civil Rights Act and past HUD settlements [2]. Furthermore, WIOA case notes are subject to FOIA [3]. Therefore, case notes must be fact-based, objective, and strictly exclude medical information or unnecessary personal trauma narratives [3]. Consent in Unite Us must be explicit and signed by the individual or legal guardian [9].
