# Richmond’s Immigrant and Refugee Services: What Works, What’s Missing, What to Do Next

## Executive Summary

Richmond’s public-facing ecosystem for immigrant and refugee services presents a mix of highly accessible, multi-lingual safety nets and fragmented, hard-to-navigate bottlenecks. Healthcare access is a distinct strength, anchored by providers like Bon Secours Care-A-Van, CrossOver Healthcare Ministry, and Daily Planet Health Services, which collectively offer robust interpreter services and integrated care. Conversely, housing assistance is currently in flux, with key eviction diversion programs shifting administrators and funding sources. 

While Spanish-language support is explicitly integrated into many organizational workflows (such as the Sacred Heart Center and CrossOver), coverage for Arabic, Nepali, and Burmese communities relies heavily on generalized interpreter lines and grassroots community associations. Furthermore, critical service categories—specifically immigration-competent legal aid, ESL/literacy programs, and workforce development—lack consolidated, public-facing entry points. To improve outcomes, stakeholders must standardize referral scripts, leverage city-backed language access tools, and formalize partnerships with trusted community associations to bridge the navigation gap.

## Purpose and Scope — Build a practical, verified map to increase successful referrals

This report organizes Richmond’s immigrant and refugee service ecosystem into an action-ready directory. By mapping 10 critical organization categories, identifying language capacities, and documenting intake requirements, this analysis highlights both the strengths of the current safety net and the gaps that cause referrals to fail. The ultimate goal is to transition from a passive list of resources to an active, verified navigation strategy that reduces dead ends for vulnerable residents.

## Citywide Access Enablers — Language access and navigation touchpoints

City-backed language access and centralized navigation platforms are high-leverage entry points that must be embedded into front-line workflows. The City of Richmond provides free interpretation and translation services to residents accessing city services [1]. This is a critical, system-level lever that is often underutilized in community navigation. 

Additionally, centralized platforms like Help1RVA have proven effective when paired with bilingual staff. During the COVID-19 pandemic, the city launched a rent and mortgage assistance program for non-citizen and mixed-status households, utilizing Help1RVA and the bilingual staff of the Office of Multicultural Affairs (OMA) to determine eligibility and complete verifications [2]. 

**Actionable Takeaway:** Train front-line navigators to open every city-facing case with a language access request. Co-design joint intake clinics with OMA at high-traffic sites (e.g., libraries, community centers) and standardize navigator scripts to include Help1RVA routing.

## Healthcare Providers with Immigrant/Refugee Focus — Strong, multi-lingual safety net

Three anchor providers cover the majority of medical needs for uninsured and Limited English Proficiency (LEP) clients in Richmond. Aligning referrals and eligibility screening across these three entities will drastically reduce missed access points.

| **Bon Secours Care-A-Van** | Free mobile clinic; primary, pediatric, women's, and behavioral health; ~14,000 visits/year [3]. | Interpreters available in over 150 languages [3]. | Uninsured/underinsured. Same-day appointments booked between 7:00 AM - 8:30 AM. Minors require guardian with photo ID [3]. | 804-545-1923 (Appointments); 804-545-1920 (General) [3]. |
| **CrossOver Healthcare Ministry** | Primary, specialty, dental, OB, mental health, and on-site charitable pharmacy [4]. | Spanish website available; bilingual staff implied [4] [5]. | Uninsured at or below 200% FPL, or Medicaid enrolled. Financial screening required [5]. | 804-655-2794 [4]. |
| **Daily Planet Health Services** | Walk-in medical care, primary care, behavioral health, MAT, dental, case management [6]. | Language assistance services provided at the health center [6]. | Serves all regardless of financial, housing, or insurance status [6]. | 804-783-2505 (24/7 after-hours line) [6]. |

**Key Takeaways:** Healthcare access is relatively strong, but intake mechanics can quietly exclude clients. Bon Secours requires calling within a strict 90-minute morning window [3], while CrossOver warns of multi-week waits for uninsured financial screenings [5]. 
**Action:** Create SMS-based appointment reminders timed to booking windows, pre-collect guardian documentation, and maintain Daily Planet as a "backup" walk-in pathway when specialty access is delayed. Centralize medication fulfillment for the uninsured through CrossOver’s pharmacy, which already dispenses to Care-A-Van and other clinics [4].

## Housing and Rental Assistance — Who to call now and what’s changed

Housing assistance in Richmond is fragmented and time-sensitive. The "where to call" guidance has recently shifted, making accurate referral routing critical to prevent evictions.

| Program / Line | Focus Area | Eligibility & Notes | Contact |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **SCDHC Eviction Diversion** | Eviction prevention | Richmond's Eviction Diversion Program is now administered by Southside Community Development & Housing Corp [9]. | Via SCDHC [9]. |
| **HOME of VA** | Eviction diversion | **No longer accepting new intake clients** for direct financial assistance due to depleted funding [10]. | Redirects to Housing Resource Line [10]. |
| **Housing Resource Line** | General housing services | Phone-based access for stable rental housing, legal aid, emergency funds (excluding imminent homelessness) [11]. | 804-422-5061 (M-F 8:30 AM - 4:30 PM) [11]. |
| **Homeless Connection Line** | Imminent homelessness | For households currently homeless or within 3 days of losing housing [11]. | 804-972-0813 (Extended weekend hours) [11]. |
| **City Rent Relief (OSF Grant)** | Non-citizen rent relief | Past program funded by a $250,000 grant providing up to $1,500 or 2 months rent for non-citizens/mixed-status families [2]. | Administered via OMA and Help1RVA [2]. |

**Key Takeaways:** The transition of eviction diversion from HOME of VA to SCDHC [9] [10] means old referral materials are likely causing dead ends. Furthermore, the COVID-era immigrant rent relief program proved that status-agnostic funding works [2], but it is not indicated as ongoing.
**Action:** Update all referral materials to route City of Richmond renters first to SCDHC, then to the Housing Resource Line. Re-create a targeted, status-agnostic rent relief microfund with philanthropic partners using the prior OMA/Help1RVA operating model.

## Legal Aid, ESL, and Workforce Development — Critical Gaps in Public Data

Based on publicly available ecosystem data, there is a severe lack of consolidated, public-facing information for three critical categories:
1. **Legal Aid for Immigrant Residents:** Public-facing, immigration-competent legal aid entries (e.g., asylum, family petitions, removal defense) are currently under-documented.
2. **ESL and Literacy Programs:** High expected demand, but missing a consolidated view of providers, schedules, and child care availability.
3. **Workforce Development:** No verified immigrant-focused workforce programs (e.g., credentialing support, ESL-integrated training) are prominently captured in general searches.

**Action:** Launch a 30-day research sprint to validate legal, ESL, and workforce pathways. Pull Help1RVA listings specifically tagged for these services and verify hotline numbers and intake windows.

## Faith-Based Organizations — Convert trusted venues into service hubs

Faith-based organizations are already embedded in the service delivery model. CrossOver Healthcare Ministry operates as a charitable clinic [4], and Bon Secours Care-A-Van explicitly partners with area churches to provide mobile health services [3]. 

**Action:** Identify congregations with high immigrant attendance and co-schedule mobile clinics, legal clinics, and ESL sign-ups at these trusted venues.

## Language Capacity Map — Spanish strong; test depth for Arabic/Nepali/Burmese

Language coverage in Richmond is uneven. Spanish resources are explicit and highly visible: CrossOver features dedicated "En Español" pages [4] [5], Sacred Heart Center focuses on Latino families [12], and the City provides translated SNAP/Public Charge documents [14]. 

However, coverage for Arabic, Nepali, and Burmese communities is implied mainly via third-party interpreter services (e.g., Bon Secours' 150+ language interpreters [3]) and the presence of community associations [18] [17]. 

**Action:** Conduct "mystery-shop" calls to QA interpreter access for Arabic, Nepali, and Burmese at major providers. Recruit bilingual navigators from the aforementioned community associations to close non-clinical navigation gaps.

## Documentation and Intake Requirements Snapshot — Prevent surprises that derail access

Intake mechanics are the primary point of failure for immigrant referrals. 
* **Time Windows:** Bon Secours Care-A-Van same-day appointments are only granted if called between 7:00 AM and 8:30 AM [3].
* **Identification:** Minors at Care-A-Van must bring a parent/guardian with photo ID and/or proof of guardianship [3]. Northside Outreach Center requires valid ID and proof of residency [13].
* **Financial Proof:** CrossOver requires a financial screening appointment to prove income is at or below 200% FPL, which can take several weeks to schedule [5].

**Action:** Create pre-intake document checklists in multiple languages. Equip navigators with alternative pathways when clients lack specific IDs or miss narrow appointment windows.

## Gaps and Priority Fixes — What’s underserved and how to close it

The largest current gaps in Richmond's public-facing ecosystem are:
1. **Adult Immigrant Mental Health:** Lack of explicit, culturally tailored adult trauma programs.
2. **Housing Financial Aid:** The depletion of HOME of VA's financial assistance [10] leaves a massive void for eviction prevention.
3. **Information Voids:** Legal aid, ESL, and workforce development lack clear, centralized entry points.

**Action:** Pilot an immigrant trauma lane with existing health partners. Rebuild the rent relief microfund. Consolidate ESL and legal aid data into a single, translated public directory.

## Richmond-Specific Directories to Pull Next — Help1RVA and rubric mentions

To fully round out this ecosystem map, two specific Richmond sources require deeper extraction:

## Implementation Plan — From map to measurable access

Pairing this directory with active navigation tools is the only way to improve access. 
1. **Build and Translate Scripts:** Create a 1-page cheat sheet for navigators detailing the 7:00 AM Care-A-Van window [3], the CrossOver pharmacy [4], and the SCDHC housing line [9].
2. **Launch Community Connectors:** Fund the Nepali and Bhutanese associations [18] [17] to act as official referral nodes.
3. **Track Outcomes:** Measure time-to-appointment and successful resolution rates by language to continuously refine the referral pathways.
