# User Journey Maps - Thriving and Inclusive Communities

Note: Journeys are constructed from rubric working session observations and general knowledge of service navigation patterns for immigrant and refugee populations. They have not been verified against specific Richmond resident interviews. Each friction point should reference an `evidence_log.md` entry when verified.

---

## Journey 1 — Recent Immigrant Family Trying to Find Rental Assistance Without Risking Exposure

**User**: A family that arrived from Central America 8 months ago. Primary language is Spanish. One adult has basic English; the other has very little. They have two children in Richmond public schools. They are undocumented.

**User goal**: Find rental assistance because they are two months behind on rent and facing eviction.

**Emotional baseline**: High anxiety. Deep fear that contacting any government-linked organization could result in immigration enforcement. Has heard from a neighbor that "you have to give your papers" to get help.

**Steps attempted**:

1. Asks a neighbor who has been in Richmond longer. Neighbor says "go to Help1RVA" but doesn't know the URL or how to use it. Describes it as "a website."

2. Searches Google in Spanish: "ayuda renta Richmond Virginia." Returns a mix of results — some from Help1RVA, some from unrelated national sites, some outdated listings.

3. Lands on Help1RVA. The interface is primarily in English. Attempts to use it but does not understand the category structure. Is afraid the "search" will require login or personal information.

4. Tries to call one of the numbers listed for a housing nonprofit. No one answers. Leaves a voicemail in Spanish. Gets no callback.

5. Asks a teacher at the children's school if they know of resources. Teacher refers to the school counselor. Counselor knows about one local organization but not how to navigate Help1RVA.

6. After two weeks with no resolution, stops actively searching. Decides to ask church community instead.

7. A member of the faith community knows of one specific organization that helps without asking for immigration papers. Provides a direct phone number for a specific person.

8. The family calls that number and eventually gets connected to rental assistance.

**Friction points**:
- Help1RVA is not multilingual or easy to navigate for someone with limited English and no prior experience with the platform
- No visible privacy statement or signal that immigration status will not be asked or shared
- Phone numbers listed on directory sites are often outdated or unstaffed
- The path to help was through a trusted person, not a technology tool
- The journey took weeks, not hours

**Prototype opportunities**:
- Multilingual service finder that opens with an explicit privacy statement: "We don't ask for your name, address, or papers"
- Simple category navigation (images + plain language) rather than keyword search
- Direct display of organizations that explicitly serve undocumented residents
- Integration with WhatsApp so residents can query through a trusted channel
- Plain-language explainer of what "calling for help" does and does not mean

## Journey 2 — Nonprofit Case Manager Spending 3 Hours Coordinating Services for One Client

**User**: A case manager at a mid-sized nonprofit that serves refugee and immigrant residents. She has a caseload of 40 clients. She has been in this role for 18 months.

**User goal**: Connect a client who recently arrived as a refugee — single mother, three children, Burmese-speaking — to housing stability support, a workforce program, and English language classes simultaneously.

**Emotional baseline**: Stressed. Knows the services exist but knows the coordination process is painful. Has learned the system by trial and error over 18 months. Worried about her client who is in a fragile situation.

**Steps taken**:

1. Checks Help1RVA for housing assistance options. Finds 4 organizations listed. Two have outdated phone numbers. One requires a referral from Social Services that the client does not have.

2. Calls Social Services to ask about the referral process. Is put on hold for 22 minutes. Is told the referral requires a completed intake form. Intake form must be completed in person at a specific office. Office has no available appointments for two weeks.

3. Checks FindHelp (through her organization's account) for workforce programs. Finds three options. One requires English proficiency the client does not yet have. One is closed to new enrollments. One looks promising.

4. Calls the promising workforce program. They do not have a Burmese interpreter on staff. She agrees to provide interpretation support during intake — adding time to her own schedule.

5. Searches online for Burmese-language English classes in Richmond. Finds partial information. Calls the library system to ask about ESL classes. Is transferred twice before reaching the right contact.

6. Documents everything in her organization's internal case management system. This system does not share data with Social Services, the workforce program, or the library.

7. Total time spent: approximately 3 hours to identify and initiate referrals for one client. She has 39 other clients.

**Friction points**:
- Help1RVA data is outdated; organization listings do not indicate language capacity
- No single view of what each organization requires for intake
- No way to know which programs have current capacity without calling each one
- No shared coordination layer — every organization has its own intake; client must re-explain their situation at each
- Language access at service providers is inconsistent and not easily searchable
- The case manager is performing coordination work that adds hours to her week

**Prototype opportunities**:
- Staff-facing service directory with language capacity indicators and current enrollment status
- Referral status tracker that shows what intake steps have been completed across organizations
- "What does this organization need for intake?" explainer — so case managers can prepare clients before calling
- Shared referral note tool (without PII) that lets organizations see what coordination has already happened
- Quick reference guide to Burmese (and other language) interpreter resources in Richmond
