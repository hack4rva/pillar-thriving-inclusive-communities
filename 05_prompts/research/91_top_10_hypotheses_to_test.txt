Top 10 Hypotheses to Test — Thriving and Inclusive Communities

For each hypothesis, assess whether it is Supported, Unsupported, or Inconclusive based on available public evidence. Cite sources for any evidence found.

1. WhatsApp is the dominant digital communication channel for Spanish-speaking immigrant communities in Richmond, more so than email, Facebook, or dedicated apps.

2. Residents who know their immigration status is not required to access a service are significantly more likely to contact that service than residents who are uncertain.

3. The primary barrier to service access for immigrant residents in Richmond is not lack of services but inability to find and navigate to the right service safely.

4. Help1RVA's current interface is not accessible to residents with limited English proficiency or limited digital literacy without assistance from a navigator.

5. Most Richmond-area service providers that serve immigrant and refugee populations have Spanish-speaking staff, but fewer than 25% have staff who speak Arabic, Nepali, or Burmese.

6. Case managers at Richmond nonprofits spend a significant portion of their working hours on coordination and referral activities that could be reduced with better information tools.

7. Cross-agency client data sharing between City departments in Richmond is blocked primarily by HIPAA and organizational policy, not by technical limitations.

8. Immigrant and refugee residents in Richmond are more likely to act on service information received from a trusted community member than from a website or government communication.

9. Tools designed for general populations (Help1RVA, 211) fail to signal privacy protections prominently enough for residents with precarious immigration status to feel safe using them.

10. A simple, no-login, multilingual service finder with an explicit privacy statement would meaningfully increase service access among Richmond's immigrant and refugee population if promoted through community ambassadors.
