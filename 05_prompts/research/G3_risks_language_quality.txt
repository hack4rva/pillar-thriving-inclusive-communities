Risks G3 — Mistranslation Risks in Multilingual Tools

Investigate the specific risks of deploying low-quality multilingual content in a service navigation tool for immigrant and refugee communities.

Risk scenarios:
1. Machine-translated service descriptions that misrepresent eligibility requirements — resident contacts wrong organization, wastes time and trust
2. Machine-translated phone numbers or addresses that are formatted incorrectly — resident cannot call or navigate to the correct location
3. Machine translation that produces culturally inappropriate or offensive language — erodes trust in the tool and the organization behind it
4. Machine translation of legal or procedural language that is technically inaccurate — resident makes decisions based on incorrect guidance
5. Translation that does not account for regional variation in Spanish (Castellano vs. Latin American Spanish varieties) — content is technically correct but feels foreign to the target community

Research:
1. What are documented cases of civic or social service tool harm from mistranslation?
2. What accuracy level does Google Translate, DeepL, or similar tools achieve for Spanish, Arabic, Nepali, and Burmese in civic/service contexts? What error types are most common?
3. What is the minimum human review process that would make machine-translated content acceptably accurate for this use case?
4. What types of content should never be machine-translated without human review? (Eligibility requirements, legal rights, emergency contacts)

Practical guidance for hackathon teams:
1. What is the minimum viable multilingual approach that is responsible?
2. How should machine-translated content be labeled in the UI?
3. Who should review translations before a tool is shown to community members?

Output:
- Risk register for translation failures
- Required vs. nice-to-have human review steps
- Content categories ranked by translation risk level
- Recommended disclaimers for machine-translated content
