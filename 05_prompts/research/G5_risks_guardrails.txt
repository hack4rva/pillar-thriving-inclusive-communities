Risks G5 — Privacy-by-Design Principles, Consent, and Transparency

Synthesize privacy-by-design principles for tools serving immigrant and refugee communities. This prompt provides the actionable guardrail framework for all prototype work in this pillar.

Privacy-by-design principles (research and adapt for this context):
1. Proactive not reactive — privacy is designed in from the start, not bolted on
2. Privacy as the default — the most protective settings are the defaults
3. Privacy embedded into design — not separated as a legal add-on
4. Full functionality — privacy does not require sacrificing functionality
5. End-to-end security — data protected throughout its lifecycle
6. Visibility and transparency — users know what is collected and why
7. Respect for user privacy — user-centric design

For each principle, provide:
- What it means in the specific context of a Richmond service navigation tool
- A concrete design decision that implements the principle
- A common anti-pattern to avoid

Consent in this context:
1. What does meaningful consent look like for a resident with limited English proficiency, limited digital literacy, and potential fear of the tool itself?
2. Is consent to data collection even appropriate for this population, or should the better design be "collect nothing at all"?
3. How do you get consent for content updates in a messenger tool (ambassador subscribes; resident receives message)?

Transparency requirements:
1. What must a privacy statement for this type of tool include?
2. How should the privacy statement be displayed — full text, summary, or layered?
3. What language level is appropriate for the primary user?

Output:
- Privacy-by-design checklist for hackathon teams
- Draft privacy statement (plain language, 200 words maximum, English and Spanish)
- Consent model recommendation for each MVP shape
- What requires legal review before deployment
