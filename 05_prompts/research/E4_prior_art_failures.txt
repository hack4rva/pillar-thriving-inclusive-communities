Prior Art E4 — Tools That Failed Due to Trust and Privacy Issues

Research documented cases of civic or social service tools that failed — specifically due to trust, privacy, or community adoption failures — in immigrant and refugee contexts. Learning from failure is as important as learning from success.

Failure patterns to investigate:
1. Tools that required account creation or personal information and saw dramatically lower adoption among undocumented or status-uncertain immigrants
2. Tools that used machine translation and delivered inaccurate or harmful guidance to users
3. Tools that were built without community input and were rejected or ignored by the communities they were meant to serve
4. Tools that collected data that was later accessed by law enforcement (even if unintentionally), eroding community trust
5. Tools that were built for a hackathon or pilot and then abandoned, leaving communities without resources they had come to depend on
6. Tools that positioned themselves as eligibility checkers and delivered incorrect guidance
7. Government-sponsored tools that were perceived as surveillance or data collection by immigrant communities

For each documented failure:
- What was the tool?
- What was the failure mode?
- What was the community impact?
- What design or process change could have prevented it?

Output:
- Documented failure cases (labeled as Verified, Inference, or Unknown)
- Common failure patterns with explanations
- Design and process guardrails that address each failure pattern
- Questions a hackathon team should ask themselves to avoid these failures
